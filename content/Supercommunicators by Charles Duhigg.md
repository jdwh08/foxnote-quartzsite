### Notes:

**Conversation Types**
The author claims that there are three broad types of conversations. 
1. Practical: related to solving problems.
2. Emotional: related to venting, expressing feelings, and processing with those feelings mean.
3. Social identity: related to finding interpersonal identities.

All people have times where they will think all three types of conversation. However, sometimes people differ based on which types of communication they send to others, and which they keep internally. The classic example matches a problem solver with an emotional venter.

In order to have a good conversation with a different person, you need to understand which conversation type you are currently engaging in, and have both people aligned on the type of conversation.

**Fights:**
In the love doctors study, researchers noticed that for some relationships, fights strengthen the relationship, while for others, the relationship slowly degrades.
The researchers centered on the idea of control as the primary differentiator between healthy and unhealthy fights. In unhealthy fights, both parties are trying to control the other person. For example, "if only you would..."
Control isn't issue because people do not like feeling controlled, however both people also want an instinct for control.
What do you do instead?
1. Control the environment. Try to mutually agree on a different time or place to talk.
2. Control yourself. Give yourself some time before you respond in order to be sure what you were saying is what you want to be saying.
3. Control a scope of the argument. One thing that tends to happen in bad marriages is "kitchen sinking", in which I fight about a small thing (e.g., where to go to dinner) escalates into a fight about much broader issues.
Try and reframe the discussion to be cooperative on what is controlled.

**Looping for Understanding**
How do you help produce productive conversations when there is conflict? Use "Looping for Understanding". 
You need to prove to the other person that you are listening to them, instead having an idea of waiting to speak and ignoring them. 
1. Ask a question, particularly a "deep question".
2. Rewrite using your own words.
3. Ask if you got the rephrasing right.
If you prove that you are listening, the other person is much more likely to listen to you.
This also ensures that you are actually listening to them.

**Deep Questions**
Ask about values, beliefs, feelings, or experiences; not merely the facts.
- "I'm a lawyer." -> "Oh, what made you decide to go to law school?" / "What do you love about it?" / "Would you tell your kids to be a lawyer?"
You can infer values, have a meaningful conversation, and this prompts "reciprocal vulnerability".

Shallow questions tend to be more factual, like in a survey. 
- "Where do you live/work", "What are your hobbies?"


**Persuasion**
Derek thinks that moral foundations will not change in a debate. Instead, points should demonstrate how your proposal fits into their moral values.
(Study found that this approach in politics gives a 5-6% influence rate.)
You can't try and "put yourself in their shoes" because this doesn't tend to work well. Instead...
1. Be genuine and curious 
2. Ask questions 
3. Loop for understanding.
4. Relate your things with them.

Other examples: CDC for COVID vaccination.
- Start off with antivax, move to other identifies like being a parent, going to the same park, etc.
- These identities sometimes conflict with each other. If you pigeonhole one identity and declare it bad, then you're going nowhere. Instead, elicit an emotional or even better identity conversation.
